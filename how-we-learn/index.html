How We Learn

The Surprising Truth About When, Where, and Why It Happens

by Benedict Carey

From InstaRead

Overview

How We Learn: The Surprising Truth About When, Where, and Why It Happens (2015) by Benedict Carey investigates the conditions under which the brain learns best. Carey provides counterintuitive advice on how to modify study habits and improve performance.

Students are commonly taught that the best way to study is to establish a routine, often by finding a quiet space, focusing intensely for a long stretch of time, and repeatedly poring over the information to be memorized. They’re taught that forgetting and distraction are antithetical to learning, and that cramming is effective. However, according to decades of research in cognitive science, much of what is taken for granted about learning is wrong.

Learning works best when it is messy, interrupted, forgotten, mixed up. Techniques that force the brain to work harder to retrieve information are most effective; they reinforce memories. One of these techniques is spacing out study sessions, so that the brain forgets a little and has to exert more effort to recall material. Another way to challenge the brain is to combine different problem types within a specific subject in one study session so that a student isn’t solving problems by rote. For example, in order to prepare for a final geometry exam, a student might alternately study angles, circles, and planes in one sitting, instead of studying each in a separate session. Changing up study locations can help to create multiple environmental associations with the studied material, which builds a stronger neural network around the memories.

Some learning techniques, by contrast, take advantage of the brain’s subconscious processing power. Taking breaks, whether for a few minutes or a few days, allows the brain to subconsciously make connections and eliminate distractions, which propels understanding and creativity forward without effort. Sleeping works in much the same way; material learned throughout the day is synthesized at night as a student dreams.

Perceptual learning also happens effortlessly. The body’s sensory system picks up cues that help it learn to distinguish patterns and categories, whether visual, auditory, or otherwise. This type of learning can be useful in many contexts, from chemistry to music to art history.

As it turns out, a less rigorous study regimen, complete with naps and long breaks, appears to work better in the long run than retreating to the library stacks for days on end. A more disorganized study routine, if it can be called a routine, also helps establish and maintain a balanced life.

Key Insight 1

Forgetting helps the brain learn.

Forgetting strengthens learning. It not only filters out extraneous information, it allows some breakdown of the neural connections that make up a memory. This is useful because subsequent retrieval of that memory requires more mental work, which further cements the memory in place. For this reason, cramming can be counterproductive; it may work for test day, but afterwards, that material is less likely to be retained.

Forgetting is not only helpful in reinforcing memories, it may also aid the creative thinking process, according to a 2014 study published in the Journal of Experimental Psychology. In this study, participants were given the name of a household object and a list of possible uses for that object. For example, a newspaper may be used as gift wrap, as kindling, as a tablecloth, and so on. In one exercise, participants were asked only to study the different uses. In another, they were instructed to study the uses and then come up with new uses for the object. After both exercises, participants were shown just the name of the object and were asked to recite the original uses. The researchers found that when participants had to come up with new purposes, they then forgot some of the original usages. Of the participants who had to devise new uses, those who forgot more of the original uses came up with new ones that were deemed more creative. The researchers concluded that forgetting may play an important role in creative thinking by allowing less useful information to fade away, so that it does not get in the way of new ideas.

Key Insight 2

Varying the study location or making changes within the study environment helps students retain information.

Research has shown that it’s easier for people to remember material they’re tested in the same place where they learned the material. This is because the information intertwines itself into the environment in which they’re working—into the sights, smells, and sounds. Although it’s usually impossible to control the testing environment, a student can manipulate the studying environment to improve learning. Multiplying the number of perceptions associated with the information creates a stronger neural web around the memory of that information. This can be accomplished by varying the circumstances in which people study, whether it be location, time of day, or study method. This disconnects the studied material from a single physical context.

A study surveying elementary school classrooms in the United Kingdom over the 2011-12 and 2012-13 school years found that the design of a classroom can significantly impact academic performance. The study, published in the journal Environment and Behavior, rated each classroom on 10 different design factors measuring parameters such as naturalness of the setting, flexibility of the environment, and level of visual stimulation. Researchers collected data on students’ progress in reading, writing, and math. They found that the quantity of natural light and the amount of flexibility in a classroom—that is, how well it could adapt to changes in curriculum—were important for student progress in all three subjects. In reading and writing, color as well as room layout and wall displays were important to learning; it was found that mid-level visual stimulation created an environment conducive to these tasks. How well a classroom was connected to nature affected writing progress. Increased access to views of nature, wooden furniture, and plants within the classroom correlated with better student performance. Overall, classroom environment accounted for 16 percent of the variation in student performance. Students of any age can apply some of these lessons to their own practice. Finding a study space with plenty of natural light, a view of the outdoors, and pleasing, moderately colorful design is sure to help facilitate learning.

Key Insight 3

Spacing out periods of study over time increases the amount of material the brain remembers.

One of the most effective learning techniques is to space out study or practice periods. Allowing time between study sessions creates space for the student both to forget and establish mental associations, techniques that, as mentioned above, help learning. Spacing out study periods can double the amount of information people retain later on. Reviewing new material immediately after it has been learned, on the other hand, does little to reinforce that information, and cramming allows few associations to develop.

Leaving time between study periods also helps a student avoid the stress associated with cramming. Stress has variable effects on memory, depending on where in the learning process the stress occurs. Research shows that stress right before or after the encoding, or introduction, of new information can benefit memory function. However, stress before testing—such as anxiety caused by cramming—can impair memory retrieval. Stress may also inhibit the integration of new knowledge with existing knowledge, preventing a student from developing a profound understanding of course material. Changes in learning and memory due to stress can also lead to mental disorders such as depression. In order to avoid cramming and the mental taxation it causes, it is better to start studying early and continue to do a small amount of review here and there.

Key Insight 4

Recognizing one’s ignorance can prompt more effective studying.

Ignorance is generally regarded as a negative characteristic. However, being able to assess one’s level of ignorance can be useful when attempting to learn new information. This is not always easy. Reviewing notes, for example, can create a feeling of mastery; because the answer is right in front of students, they feel like they know the material. Self-testing, which might involve using flashcards or reciting from memory, can dispel this illusion and help students evaluate what areas need improvement. Self-examination works far better than simply rereading notes because it forces the brain to work hard to recall information, and the harder the brain works to find it, the more tightly that information is stored. Even guessing at answers strengthens the brain’s hold on the correct answer.

Acceptance of one’s relative ignorance—also called intellectual humility—has other benefits. In addition to motivating study, intellectual humility makes people more open to new ideas, according to a 2014 study published in the Journal of Positive Psychology. Tenelle Porter, a Stanford University researcher on intellectual humility, has further indicated that it leads to better grades. Porter and her colleagues found that intellectual humility creates an openness to learning about different sides of social issues such as capital punishment and affirmative action, as well as an ability to engage in civil discussion with people of opposing political views. [5] There is an additional connection between intellectual humility and wisdom, according to a 2012 study published in the Journal of Experimental Psychology. The authors of this study defined wisdom as the ability to understand the limits of one’s own knowledge, the recognition that the future is likely to change, and the tendency to be other-oriented rather than self-oriented. As Cindy Lamothe at The Cut has pointed out, attaining wisdom requires humans to move beyond the typical belief that they are superior to others in almost any area; psychologists call this the superiority illusion. Acceptance of one’s own ignorance can lead not only to better studying, but to a richer life.

Key Insight 5

When a student is stumped by a particularly tough problem, taking a short break allows the brain to work on the problem subconsciously.

One effective learning technique is to step away from the material for five to 20 minutes when the brain is stuck on a certain problem. A short break allows the brain to continue to work on the problem subconsciously. The brain may do this by free associating, by dispensing with an unhelpful assumption the student had fixated on, or by forgetting distracting information.

The fact that the brain can profit from intermittent breaks shows that digital distraction may not be as harmful as is commonly believed. However, this assertion comes with an important caveat. Taking a pause when stuck is not the same as checking texts, Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter every two minutes. Research has shown that this kind of distraction may be harmful to learning. A 2015 study found that people who use their cell phones and the internet heavily are more prone to forget things, have poor spatial awareness, and tune out when someone is talking to them. The author of the study notes that it is not clear if correlation in this case indicates causation. Another study by researchers at the University of Texas published in 2017 found that the mere presence of a smartphone markedly decreases cognitive capacity. Technology addiction can also lead to fatigue, which is detrimental to learning. By its very nature, digital wandering can be endless. According to Josh Davis, the director of research at the NeuroLeadership Institute in New York, the type of information people take in through their smartphones and the internet is often upsetting, such as a friend’s Facebook post that causes jealousy, news about a war that induces anxiety, or the actions of a political leader that are viewed as depressing. The combined effect of all these stimuli can emotionally wear out someone who should be saving mental energy for studying.

Key Insight 6

If a student is working on a long-term creative project, prolonged breaks can prove useful because the brain takes in and processes relevant information in the meantime.

For creative projects in particular, downtime of a few hours or days is often necessary for nascent ideas and strategies to germinate and bear fruit. Once an artist or writer or designer or builder is involved in a project, that project begins to occupy the forefront of that person’s mind—even when the person is interrupted. The brain begins to make connections between the outside world and the project even during off-time, thus enlarging the pool of ideas and associations that can be drawn upon when the person goes back to work. The benefit of longer breaks during a creative project means that it is best to start early on such an endeavor and to allow for intermittent procrastination.

Research has found that there are other ways to spark creative thinking. In fact, brains can also be trained to produce creative ideas. James Webb, the author of A Technique for Producing Ideas (2016), told Fast Company in 2013 that new ideas are produced by combining old elements in a new way. He suggests gathering inspiring bits of information during the day and storing them in a scrapbook or on index cards. Each day, Webb says, the person should set aside some time in a relaxing spot where the brain can experiment with creating relationships between elements in the scrapbook. After this practice, the person can let all these creative thoughts go and allow the subconscious mind to work its magic.

Studies have found that many other unexpected factors can contribute to creativity. Dim lighting, for one. One 2013 study found that low lighting decreases inhibition and provokes an exploratory state of mind. Other research has found that moderate amounts of alcohol can promote creativity, as can the color green, dream recall, a positive mood, and listening to Vivaldi. Combined with breaks, these tricks can propel a project forward when a creative person is facing a block.

Key Insight 7

Studying different subjects within the same discipline at the same time strengthens retention.

Repeated review of the same information seems like a tried-and-true method for retention, but research shows that varying the type of material studied within the same subject improves mastery over the longer term. For example, studying the structures of atoms, molecules, and compounds at the same time, as opposed to one at a time, helps cement retention of all three subjects. Retention is stronger because the task is more difficult; instead of going into the task already knowing what chemical structures will be addressed, the student must decipher between various structures to complete problems. This strategy improves a student’s ability to apply skills learned through one task to another.

After How We Learn was published, a new study that tested this type of problem-mixing in a real-world setting was published. The 2015 study, which appeared in the Journal of Educational Psychology, surveyed seventh-graders in Tampa, Florida who were learning algebra and geometry. Over three months, weekly homework assignments were either “blocked,” meaning they featured problems of the same type, or “interleaved,” meaning they contained a mix of problem types. The day after the last lesson, the students were tested. Those who studied interleaved problems performed 25 percent better than those trained with blocked problems. One month later, those trained on mixed problem sets performed 76 percent better.

Interleaving is better for retention not only because it forces the brain to distinguish between concepts, but also because it creates more associations among memories in the brain. Interleaving requires students to constantly retrieve strategies for different tasks rather than relying on one strategy, In so doing, it strengthens the neural connections around the correct answer.

Key Insight 8

Perceptual learning—the ability of the sensory system to learn from its environment to improve its response to stimuli—can be deployed to improve performance in various subjects and skills.

In addition to physical and intellectual learning, a third form of learning exists called perceptual learning. The brain can automatically pick up subtle visual, auditory, olfactory, and sensory cues that help the brain perceive better. For example, the more often musicians hear different pitches in music, the more easily they are able to distinguish between them. The more often doctors examine x-rays, the more likely they are to be able to identify a tumor in an x-ray. Recognizing these patterns becomes intuitive. Perceptual learning can aid in the study of a variety of subjects. If students are studying chemical bonds, for instance, they can create slides or flashcards that show the shape of various bonds. Students should then flip through these slides or cards as quickly as possible, letting their sensory system pick up and identify the shapes and their corresponding bond type.

A 2017 study published in the journal Instructional Science elaborated on how best to employ perceptual learning. The authors tested whether it is more effective for students working on fractions to be able to verbally explain concepts first and then move on to developing a visual instinct for those concepts through perceptual learning, or vice versa. The researchers found that the former is true: acquisition of reasoning abilities before turning to perceptual learning supported later perceptual fluency, while starting with perceptual learning did not seem to enhance reasoning abilities. This suggests that it may not be best to dive straight into perceptual learning without having an intellectual grasp on the subject matter first.

Key Insight 9

Sleep is crucial in filtering through and consolidating what is learned during the day.

Though the research on sleep as a learning aid is ongoing, the vast majority of studies so far show that sleep improves retention and comprehension of what was picked up during the day. Not only that, but each of the five stages of sleep may function to consolidate a certain type of memory. REM sleep, for example, seems to help with the recognition of patterns, problem solving, interpreting emotional memories, and detecting relationships between pieces of information that didn’t surface during the day. Stage 2 sleep is the motor memory consolidation stage. Stages 3 and 4 are important for remembering facts and events. Interestingly, napping can often provide nearly the same learning benefits as a full eight hours of sleep.

With age, the relationship between sleep and memory making deteriorates. A 2017 study in the journal Neuron examined how this happens. In young adults, the brain waves of deep sleep are synchronized perfectly. This brain wave coordination enables the saving of long-term memories at night. As humans age, they begin to lose brain cells in the part of the brain that generates deep sleep. The loss of these brain cells throws off brain wave synchronization, and thus long-term memory formation. One of the co-authors of the study, Matthew Walker, is working with colleagues to invent a technology that would apply artificial electrical stimulation to the brain to resynchronize those brain waves and improve deep sleep, and thus memory, for the elderly.
